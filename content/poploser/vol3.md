I used to know how computers work. If something broke, either inside my laptop or on my website, I could usually figure it out and fix it. It was a thing I enjoyed doing. Somewhere along the way, I stopped knowing how things work and stopped wanting to figure it out. Part of that is that I bought a bunch of products and started using websites that "just work." Part of it was just me getting older and devoting more of that time to things like learning to make bread, reading books and having a few time-sucking kids. It happens. 

Last winter, the arcade cabinet I built a couple years ago crapped out. Screen went black and it wouldn't turn on. I poked at it for about 10 minutes and then walked away, because trying to relearn how I made it work in the first place was too daunting, never mind troubleshooting software and hardware in a rigorous way. Too hard! Too much! 
One of the time-sucking children finally complained — he had been waiting months to work on his Pac-Man score. Fair enough. The easiest solution, I figured, would be to buy the same model Raspberry Pi and just swap in the SD card and plug it in, but I didn't know if that was possible. I found a forum, signed up and asked, getting a few pretty quick answers that amounted more or less to "Maybe... but it's probably not that simple." Then one guy suggested I replace the power supply — an $8 plug — because reasons (the specifics don't matter here, but I assure you every response I got was fucking <em>thorough</em>. Sure enough, that's all it was and boy did I feel stupid and lazy. Don't tell my kids. 

I hope you enjoyed your summer. Welcome to Pop Loser Vol. 3.

### <i class="fa-solid fa-skull-crossbones"></i> <i class="fa-solid fa-skull-crossbones"></i> <i class="fa-solid fa-skull-crossbones"></i>

At some point we all agreed that open access to all human knowledge was a good thing and accordingly made libraries a staple of every city, every town and every school. It was one of the rare areas we managed to mostly avoid fucking up for a really, really long time. <a href="https://openlibrary.org">Open Library</a> is, in every sense and by every definition, a library. The only real difference is that it operates at the scale of the web, which is more or less the vision of the Library of Alexandria fully realized. Woo hoo, we did it! Nice work, everybody! 

Enter <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/internet-archive-loses-hachette-books-case-appeal/">capitalism</a>; the big book publishers want it all stopped because Open Library presents "a fundamental devaluation of authors’ and publishers’ intellectual and creative investments," which U.S. courts are so far agreeing with despite it being a very stupid point. Even worse, the ruling implies that <a href="https://www.techdirt.com/2024/09/05/second-circuit-says-libraries-disincentivize-authors-to-write-books-by-lending-them-for-free/">all libraries</a> are, in fact, bad. 

I love my <a href="https://invisiblepublishing.com">publisher</a> (small publishers are great and do a lot operating in a system that constantly punishes them), but books are absolutely broken, both as a functional business model and as a means of creating and distributing art and ideas. Libraries are the last institution putting <em>value</em> on them at all. 

We really had our shit together there for a bit, didn't we?

Current internet trend: escaping the algorithm. From "<a href="https://thewalrus.ca/collapse-of-self-worth-in-the-digital-age/">the collapse of self-worth in the digital age</a>: ==❝We are not giving away our value, as a puritanical grandparent might scold; we are giving away our facility to value. When we scroll, what are we looking for?❞== 

I have sworn off being one of those smug assholes who tells you how to lifehack your media consumption, but I am now going to be one of those smug assholes who tells you how to lifehack your media consumption. 

I have a sure-fire, never-fails trick for enjoying hand-picked ("curated," if you will, though normally I would not) music — new and old — instead of being a slave to the algorithmic tyranny of the Spotibrain, and it doesn't require resorting to <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ou2dW4qebbw">silly extremes</a> because this is a problem we actually solved a long-ass time ago: <strong>listen to the fucking radio</strong>. 

<a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/sounds">BBC Sounds</a> (primarily <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/sounds/play/live:bbc_6music">BBC 6</a>, where I need to once again mention <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b03yblbx/episodes/player">Iggy Pop has his own Sunday afternoon show</a>) is the default music in my home. <a href="https://www.cbc.ca/listen/live-radio">CBC Music</a> (you'll always be "Radio 2" to me) feels like it's offering less programming than ever, but the programs they do have are quite good. <a href="http://radio.garden">Radio Garden</a> is a great way to spend an evening at home with or without drugs. Now that iPhones can play music and Shazam at the same time, you can build spectacular playlists.

Two things I strongly believe in: public media and portable content. Which is why I'm more than a little dismayed that the <a href="https://www.cbc.ca/rss/">RSS feeds</a> from CBC News stopped working over a month ago. I figured it was a known issue and they'd get to it, but then three weeks went by and I started to get an uneasy feeling. Eventually, I submitted a support ticket, but I kind of knew what the answer was going to be and I was right: ==❝Unfortunately accessing our content via RSS feed is not popular with many users and so resources are generally being allocated to other areas and fixes for glitches with current RSS feeds are not being prioritized. I'm sorry I don't have better news.❞==

Fucking hell, the internet is a goddamned disaster right now.

#### Social needia.

I don’t know, follow your heart, I guess. 

But, at the very least, be cautious about the time you’re sinking into building an audience on another privately owned service with buckets of VC cash and no current revenue model, because on a long enough timeline they will probably fuck you. I only say this because they pretty much all have fucked us — Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, Tumblr, Reddit, TikTok, etc., etc., etc. <a href="https://pluralistic.net/2024/11/02/ulysses-pact/">Cory Doctorow has thoughts</a> that are worth reading before you’re in too deep.

#### Art of bore.
I’ve been thinking about art a lot lately. Mostly it has been in a separating-art-from-artist context, which is stupid idea that I think devalues both, but it leads to a lot of existential “What even is art?” ideas. My initial response to the suggestion people can’t really tell the difference between human- and AI-made art, and, in fact, kind of prefer the AI stuff, was “Jesus fucking Christ what the fuck has happened to people’s fucking brains.” <a href="https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/how-did-you-do-on-the-ai-art-turing">Reading</a> the actual “study” and its “results” and “conclusions” only forced me to jam a few more “fucks” in. 

<a href="https://maxread.substack.com/p/people-prefer-ai-art-because-people?post_id=151984955&r=16ptr">Max Read</a>: ==❝In fact, the dental-office “badness” of so much of the A.I. art is precisely why I don’t dispute Alexander’s assertion that people preferred it. Like any LLM output, A.I.-generated images are designed to please, not to provoke. I’ve argued before that these images are, by their nature, almost unavoidably kitsch–comforting, straightforward, accessible, flattering. And people love kitsch!❞== There’s that, but also the whole thing feels designed to trick people, which is infuriating enough, but it also shows a total lack of thinking about what art is, how art is made, and what art is for. There’s another version of this using <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-76900-1">poetry</a> (further discussed in the Read link above) and fuck fucking fuck I don’t want to talk about it. 

The discussion around AI bounces around between genuinely fascinating and wildly stupid, but I don’t think we can understand it in an artistic context because we’re constantly wrestling with our relationship with art, which is kind of <em>the entire point of art</em>. Or at least <em>good</em> art, which is a whole other discussion, but probably one you want to have before you start suggesting a computer can do it at all, never mind better. 

As usual, I have no answers to suggest, just things to yell about. But I do keep coming back to <a href="https://typo.social/@Lettermin/113163089663497919">this</a> nugget.

I think there might really be something to this idea (though without the religious implications). There’s something people have that computers, at least for now and I think/hope forever, don’t. Shit is keeping me up at night.
